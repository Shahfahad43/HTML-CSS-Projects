Muhammad Ali Jinnah

1876 - 1948

Founder Of Pakistan

Muhammad Ali Jinnah, widely known as Quaid-e-Azam (meaning "Great Leader"), was the founding father of Pakistan and a pivotal figure in South Asian history. Born on December 25, 1876, in Karachi, then part of British India, Jinnah was educated at the Sindh Madrasa-tul-Islam, followed by further studies in law at Lincoln’s Inn in London. He began his political career with the Indian National Congress in the early 20th century but grew disillusioned with its increasingly Hindu-majoritarian orientation. By 1913, he joined the All-India Muslim League, becoming its most prominent leader and chief architect of the Two-Nation Theory, which held that Muslims and Hindus were distinct nations, each deserving of its own state. His leadership and legal acumen enabled him to negotiate effectively with the British and Indian National Congress, culminating in the partition of India and the creation of Pakistan on August 14, 1947. Jinnah served as Pakistan’s first Governor-General until his death on September 11, 1948. Known for his secular vision, insistence on rule of law, and commitment to democratic principles, Jinnah’s speeches—such as his landmark August 11, 1947 address to the Constituent Assembly—emphasized religious freedom, minority rights, and the separation of religion from the state. Despite deteriorating health due to tuberculosis and lung cancer, he continued to work tirelessly for the nascent nation. Today, he remains a revered figure in Pakistan and is often described as one of the most influential leaders of the 20th century.
